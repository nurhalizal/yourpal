The Light Underneath Our Eyelids

July 16, 2025 Physics of Everyday Wonder

I was under the sun on a motorbike blinking a few times, a random wonder came to my mind, “Why can we still ‘see’ some colors with our eyes closed under certain conditions?” Now, before you continue reading this, close your eyes for a few seconds and observe what you ‘see’—most likely, you’ll perceive ‘black’. But if you’re under the sun, facing the sky/sun, you might see other colors instead; red or orange. Did you ever wonder why does that happen? Cause I did. After that, I spent some more time on this wonder until I felt like I’ve built my understanding about it.

Few days following that day, I decided to share this thought with ChatGPT (yeah, this has become one of the best ones to share our thoughts about, apparently). Genuinely, I was so happy with the feedback—I got confirmations about my thoughts! So, I guess I’d love to share about it in this piece.

First thing we need to be aware of is that the light we see is a part of the electromagnetic wave spectra—which has a range of wavelengths from the longest (radio wave) to the shortest (gamma ray). The visible light’s wavelength typically ranges from 380 nm (violet) to 700 nm (red) and is the only portion our human eyes can see directly, hence the name. However, when we close our eyes, our eyelids act as a barrier that ‘blocks’ incident light—although not completely. Thus, even the visible light spectrum can’t reach our retina with our eyes closed, leaving us perceiving ‘black’ which is essentially the absence of visible light.

But even so, the interaction of electromagnetic wave and matter (such as eyelids) is not as simple as just blocking it. As electromagnetic wave travels through matter, portions of it can be reflected, absorbed, and transmitted. These typical behaviours depend on the properties of the matter and the electromagnetic wavelengths. Each material interacts differently with electromagnetic waves. Our eyes is one example of it: our eyes allow the visible light transmission into our eyes to reach our retina which is responsible for sensing light using photoreceptor cells within.

When it comes to closing our eyes, the eyelids ‘block’ light so that very little to no light reaches the retina. This is the case where all we can perceive is ‘black’. Yet, there are other cases, such as when we’re around high-intensity light. In this case, high-intensity light which obviously has high energy as well reach our eyes. Even if we close them, some portion of the light will still be penetrated through our eyelids—they’re not perfect at blocking light. Overall, human skin including eyelids tend to allow the transmission of longer wavelengths, particularly red-orange light. In other words, human skin is more transparent to these wavelengths compared to shorter wavelengths like blue and violet. In addition to it, red-orange spectrum has the longest wavelength among all visible colors, thus they’re less likely to be scattered, unlike the shorter wavelengths. As a result, when there’s intense light reaching our vision, we’ll still ‘see’ red or orange colors even with our eyes closed.

I get that this is such a random thing to wonder about, but it seems like even the most absurd observations can turn out to be one fascinating concept to think of. I guess science would never fail to amaze me. Even something as simple as seeing and blinking is an excellent instance of how everything works in such perfect way.

Other ways, our vision will be completely different, and rather unpleasant. Imagine having X-ray level, the pretty faces we see all day possibly wouldn’t appear as pleasant anymore, and we would likely be distracted by basically everything around us.
