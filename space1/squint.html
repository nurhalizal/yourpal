Why Do We Squint When It's Too Bright?

September 7, 2025 Science of Everyday Awe

If you’re under the shining sun, try to look up at the sky and see what you do—or more precisely, what your eyes do. You’ll notice that while doing so, you spontaneously squint your eyes. Well, that unsell you choose to close it whatsoever, haha. Not many people question this behaviour, but I do believe there are some who wonder, why do we squint our eyes when facing bright light?

Interestingly, eyes-squinting is a highly necessary action that does us a lot of favor. When squinting our eyes, the eyelid muscles narrow the opening of the eyelid for light to enter our eyes. As a result, the amount of light can be limited to prevent excessive light from reaching our retinas—which are light-sensitive. Then, besides avoiding overexposure, squinting our eyes also help to enhance focus. That’s why, you probably often see your friend squinting their eyes when trying to recognize you from a far. But well, sometimes one does that just to see if the other person would squint back—not because their eyes were unfocused, hehe. Anyway, now you see that squinting our eyes, especially in bright light is very important for our own sake.

However, there’s something else we need to know related to it. This mechanism plays similar role—or work together—with a more subtle eye behaviour: pupil constriction. Unlike squinting which seems much more noticeable and can be done consciously, pupil constriction is a subtler, yet even more automatic. We barely ever realize this, but our pupils work all the time to adjust to the level of brightness they perceive. In dark places, our pupils dilate, allowing more light to enter our eyes and reach the retinas. On the contrary, pupils will constrict (become narrow) as our eyes meet higher light intensity.

If you don’t believe so, check it by yourself. Face a mirror while holding a flash light and don’t forget to keep your eyes on your eyes—haha, that sounds strange, but fun. Then, slowly direct the flashlight to your eyes, but maintain a moderate distance so that you wouldn’t need to squint (or at least can hold yourself not to). There, you’ll se your pupils gradually constrict, then as you turn off the flashlight, they’ll dilate again. By the way, I’ve done that couple of times since I was told in a class that pupils work that way.

Ever since, I’ve always been fascinated about it and I may try it again anytime soon just to feel the awe again. It’s beyond amazing how an unnoticeable automatic behaviour of our organs is actually a form of protection in disguise.
