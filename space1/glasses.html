How Do Glasses Correct Vision?

July 2, 2025 Physics of Everyday Wonder

I believe we all have seen a bunch of people constantly wear glasses every day. They tend to look cool with the glasses on, don’t they? But the twist is, people who wear glasses constantly do not even wear them simply to look cool. Some of them have no choice but to do so and their glasses have corrective lenses. These spectacles help people with eye conditions, such as farsightedness (hypermetropia), and near-sightedness (myopia). But, how do eyeglasses correct vision? Now, let’s find out the answer together.

Our eyes work by capturing light and converting it as signals for the brain to read. In order for us to see clearly, light must be focused by the cornea and eye lenses so that it falls on the retina which is located in the back of the eye. Eye conditions as I mentioned happen because the light does not fall exactly on the retina—it either falls in front of the retina or at the back of it. These conditions occur due to several reasons, like the size of the eyeballs, shape of the cornea and lens, and/or the lens fail to accommodate to focus light. As a result, the vision gets blurry—thus, this is the exact reason why some people need glasses.

Corrective eyeglasses are used to solve this typical problem by compensating to the errors. The corrective lenses are carefully designed in certain curved shape which can help focusing light so that it falls exactly on the retina. Convex lenses as used by people with hypermetropia, work by refracting light so that it will converge before entering the eye. So, for people with hypermetropia, where light would normally fall behind the retina, the lens helps bring it forward to fall exactly on the retina. On the opposite, concave lenses diverge light so that it will reach the retina even though it normally would fall in front of the retina, just like how people with myopia experience. By the way, I realized that it will be so much more understandable with some illustrations. I’m probably going to add some later.

Basically, these corrective glasses are implementation of optical physics. They are designed based on the principle of light refraction. When passing through curved lenses, the light will refract or ‘bend’ as its angle of incidence varies through the surface. This results in concentrated light after passing a convex lens and diverged light after passing a concave lens. So, to put it simply, the eyeglasses work by ‘bending’ light before entering the eye, so that it will fall at the retina even if the eye lenses fail to accommodate.

Now, what’d you think of it? I personally think it’s astounding how people came up with the glasses as solutions to eye conditions as well as the physics principle behind this. But, what’s even more awesome is the fact that our eyes are indeed created to work in that precise way—capturing, controlling, and even converting light.
