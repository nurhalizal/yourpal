The Other Side Of The Rain

July 27, 2025 Science of Everyday Awe

One of the biggest class projects I’ve ever done was an Indonesian language task to make a paper about any topic we want. After weeks of struggling to pick a topic, I finally decided to write about Foehn wind and rain shadow phenomenon. Actually, the paper was not a very well written one involving scientific research—the source of the material in the paper was mainly online literature I read. But even so, I believe the process of finishing the paper project has given me worthy and interesting insights about the topic I chose.

Before I picked the topic, I’ve heard about the terms before, so I basically had read a simple overview of what the rain shadow is all about. But I guess, for someone who hasn’t heard of it before, I reckon it would give rise to a question or a sudden imagination of what a rain shadow could possibly be like. Then eventually, one may find oneself wondering, how could such thing be formed? As always, such question is something exciting for me to at least attempt on answering. This time, I’ll use my paper task as a reference.

In my paper project, I in fact focused on elaborating the formation of rain shadow area, which turned out to also answer the question oh how deserts were created. It involves wind, rain, and mountains playing their roles. To begin with, picture a mountain area divided into two sides with different characteristics. One is the mountains’ side having humid and relatively cold air which moves upward to the mountain peaks. As the wind moves upward, it begins to condensate resulting in precipitation on the same side of the mountains. This side is also known as windward side or the rain area while the opposite side is called the leeward side.

The leeward side has the complete opposite characteristic of the windward side. On this side, the ‘remaining’ wind from the windward side slides down the mountains. But as the moisture in the air has already condensed and precipitated on the windward side, it no longer has high humidity—means the leeward side is left with dry air/wind; it’s the Foehn wind. Then, as the already dry wind moves down the mountains, it receives more heat causing its temperature to rise significantly. At last, the leeward side of the mountains constantly get roundly dry and hot air.

Not just that, this side doesn’t even get exposed to the rain. At first, we may think that the precipitation on the windward side can easily reach the leeward side, yet it’s not the case here. The mountains area has undoubtingly high relief that is high enough to block the rain from the other side. That’s why, this side is what’s called as the rain shadow area.

Rain shadow areas are widespread across the earth due to the uneven relief of the earth’s surface with numerous mountain ranges. These rain shadow areas are mostly deserts with obviously arid condition and very low rainfall. Some well-known instances are, the Atacama Desert in Chile, Gobi Desert, and Death Valley in California. So, in a way, some deserts actually exist due to the rain—just on the other side. What a twist, right?

Ps: By the way, I want to include the analogous relation between rain shadow and just shadow.

Logically, we knew that shadow usually refers to the absence of light, typically when light is blocked with opaque substance. Analogously, rain shadow is the absence of rain, as it is blocked by the mountain.
