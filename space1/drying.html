Why Do Clothes Dry Faster In The Sun?

June 4, 2025 Physics of Everyday Wonder

I suppose we’re all familiar with drying things in the sun, especially clothes. Drying under the sun is apparently the best method to dry several things, including clothes as they will dry faster. But, does anyone ever wonder why clothes dry faster in the sun? Why can’t we just hang it inside and expect them to dry as quick?

I have a vague memory related to these questions from my elementary school time. In my memory, we were given a task or quiz with an illustration of a person drying clothes under the sun and we needed to explain what was going on in the picture. My recollection of my answer that time has faded, all I have left is that I mentioned something about the sunlight and probably energy. So now—years after that—I’m about to elaborate on the drying process under the sun.

The sunlight we see is a part of the electromagnetic radiation that the sun emits. This radiation is no other than energy being emitted by the sun. As this radiation reaches the earth surface, it gives thermal energy (heat) to the things it touches—and the clothes we hang outside are included. When drying wet clothes, the water molecules also absorb the sun’s thermal energy, causing their temperature to rise. At this point, the water molecules would gain enough energy to undergo a transition from the liquid to vapor or as we call it, they evaporate. Eventually, the water molecules trapped in the fabric would evaporate completely, leaving us with dry clothes.

This drying process involving the sun needs less time that it would under closed space. Besides, the wind or airflow also helps; they ‘swipe’ the water vapor away from the clothes—letting them to dry faster.

Imagine hanging your clothes inside to dry, they would neither get the sun’s energy directly nor enough airflow to dry them quicker. Well, yeah, you might be thinking of drying machine right now and the fact that the machine doesn’t need sun nor breeze. Yes, that’s true. But they actually use other form of energy; kinetic energy to ‘squeeze’ the water out of our clothes by certain motions and speed. Anyway, I don’t think drying machine can dry things completely, though. Or is it just the one I have at home that doesn’t work to dry clothes completely?

After all this elaboration, of a rather simple thing we experience, this gives us the realization of how significant the role of energy in this world. Basically, nothing can really happen without it.
