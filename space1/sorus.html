What Are Those Dots Beneath The Leaves?

June 1, 2025 Science of Everyday Awe

The kingdom Plantae is characterized by the presence of the green pigment chlorophyll, which is primarily found in leaves. There are numerous plants and they are classified into different taxonomic classification based on their distinct features. A leaf is one of a plant’s parts that identifies a lot about the plant. One of the examples is if you see leaves of a plant which have dark dots beneath them, you’re likely looking at ferns.

Ferns or also known scientifically as Pteridophyta is a phylum of Plantae aside of seed plants (Spermatophyta) and mosses (Bryophyta). This phylum is classified as vascular plants like seed plants—yet unlike seed plants—ferns do not reproduce via flowers and seeds. Instead, they reproduce using structures called spores, much like mosses do as well. Both ferns and mosses produce their spores in sporangia (plural of sporangium), but their sporangia are located in different parts.

Ferns have several different ‘storage’ of sporangia, including in a group called sorus (plural: sori). This sori/sorus is what we often see underneath the leaves of ferns. They appear in various colors, but most of the ones I’ve seen are either brown or black. These groups of sporangia will hold the spores untl they’re mature enough to be released. As the spores are then dispersed and land in suitable environment, they will grow into prothalli (plural: prothallium). Each prothallium produce egg and sperm which will fertilize and grow into a new fern plant.

So, those rather small ‘dots’ we see on the underside surface of leaves actually hold the key of the fern’s life cycle.
