Hair, Balloon, and Static Electricity

July 9, 2026 Physics of Everyday Wonder

I reckon that giving/performing simple science experiments in the classrooms is one of the best ways to teach science. This method works both to give a well understanding of the science principle and to strike students excitement. Apparently, the lesson we get from an experiment also stays better in our head. One of the only few science experiments I was ever explained to was the ‘rubbing two objects’ experiment. As I recall, my teacher invited us to rub a ruler on the surface of our clothes or hair (I don’t remember the detail), then put the rubbed ruler near small ripped pieces of paper.

The outcome of the experiment was to see that the pieces of paper will be attached to the ruler—sometimes even before the ruler was supposed to touch the paper. Yes, it was a very simple experiment, but it introduced us to a clear physics principle. Oh yeah, if you’re unfamiliar with this instance, you probably have heard or seen the balloon and hair one instead. The experiment is very much alike. If you rub a balloon and hair together, eventually when you lift the balloon from your hair, strands of the hair will be attracted to the balloon—looking like the strands of hair cling individually to the balloon.

Most of us have probably heard of the explanations from our teachers back then, but why not walk through it again this time? Both of these experiments carry the same physics theory: static electricity. Static electricity is a form of electricity that merely depends on static electrical charge—with no electric current formed/involved. As we know, every object is made of atoms, which consists of two electric charges: proton (positive charge) and electron (negative charge). Neutral atoms have balance amount of proton and electron, and so do the atoms of the objects involved in the experiments above.

However, when we rub two objects together, the interaction between surfaces causes the transfer of electrons—tiny negatively charged particles around the surface of atoms. The electron transfer leads to the two different objects becoming electrically charged in two opposite ways (no longer neutral). Object that releases its electrons will become a positively charged object, meanwhile the object that receives the electrons becomes negatively charged as it has more electrons than protons within. In the balloon-hair experiment, the hair tends to be the one that loses electrons, making it the positively charged object. Hence the balloon is negatively charged.

According to the Coulomb’s law, we know that two opposite charges will attract each other, meanwhile the same charges will form a repulsive force at each other. So, in this case, the hair and balloon are oppositely charged, thus they attract each other. Concurrently, all strands of hair are positively charged, means they naturally will repel each other. As a result, the hair strands stand up clinging to the balloon separately. But then, just in case you wonder why it stops as you pull the balloon away; it’s because Coulomb force also depends on the distance. The closer two charges are, the stronger their attractive/repulsive force becomes. When the distance is increased, the force will eventually dwindle away. So yeah, I guess this is a particle-level example of how two opposites will attract each other, yet the distance matters too, haha.

Ps: I’ve heard my lecturers/teachers explain the attractive-repulsive charge between charges in the context of human, hence my previous sentence.

Oh and I took the balloon-hair example cause I found it easier to talk/write about.
