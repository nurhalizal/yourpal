How Do We Know Our Blood Type

July 13, 2026 Science of Everyday Awe

Once upon a day when I was in kindergarten, all the students including me got their blood type tested. It was just a quick process: they pricked a ballpoint-like thing to my finger, took a drop of my blood, then like seconds later they gave me a card with my blood type written on it. Alongside my name and blood type, that card had what I assume as dried blood droplets in boxes labelled with unfamiliar codes. For a quite long time, it used to bring out the curiosity in me. Like, how could they tell my blood type simply by taking drops and placing it into this card? And wait, how do we have blood types in the first place? What makes the difference between each of them?

Years later, now I think I have found the answer to those questions—thanks to a tutor I got in middle school. So, based on the blood type system we know, a blood type is determined by the presence and absence of certain antigens in the red blood cells. There are two common blood type classification systems: the ABO and rhesus system, but I’m gonna focus on the ABO system this time.

The ABO system classifies the blood based on the presence-absence of two types of antigens, namely A and B antigens in the red blood cells. On top of that, this system also tells us about the antibody presence in the blood plasma. Let me try to give a brief breakdown of this system. Blood type A and B have A antigens and B antigens in their red blood cells while having anti-B and anti-A antibodies in their plasma respectively. Meanwhile, type AB has both A and B antigens but its plasma doesn’t contain any antibodies. On the other side, type O has no antigens, yet have both anti-A and anti-B antibodies in its plasma. Well, the concept is quite simple, isn’t it?

Now that we know how the blood types are classified generally, understanding the blood type test would be much easier. Here’s the what I’ve got so far based on the tutor’s explanation back then. It starts with the tester drawing sample of our blood then place it on a test kit, typically a card like the one I have. The sample is placed in a few different spots meant to be tested differently. After that, each droplet is mixed with different antibodies, primarily anti-A and anti-B. As the serum of blood is mixed with the antibodies, the tester will observe whether agglutination occur or not in each sample. The principle is that if antigens mix with ‘wrong’ antibodies (like antigen A mixed with anti-A antibodies), the red blood cells will clump (agglutinate), and otherwise they will remain stable.

As an instance, if the red blood cells of a sample remain stable when mixed with anti-A antibodies but clump when mixed with anti-B antibody—the blood is type B. Type B red blood cells had B antigens which ‘match’ with anti-A antibodies as its plasma naturally has these antibodies, yet they will clump as mixed with anti-B antibody. To sum this up, when testing blood type, the blood serum is mixed with anti-A and anti-B antibodies to see whether it will clump or not. If the blood only clumps with anti-A, it’s type A; if it only clumps with anti-B, it’s type B; if it clumps with both, it’s type AB; and if it doesn’t clump with either, it’s type O.

This principle also shows the answer of why knowing blood type is crucial, especially when it comes to transfusion. Blood type determination helps paramedics prevent predictable issues caused by unmatched blood transfusion. Imagine how fatal it could be if our blood mixed with other blood which has the opposite antibody our blood should never have. Overall, the blood type test mechanism probably doesn’t sound too complicated, but I guess it was indeed complicated back then for those scientists to finally come into conclusion of all these. Thanks to them, it barely takes minutes to know our blood type nowadays, and it basically prevents us from a whole transfusion problem. Looking at this, it’s a clear instance how a simple science quietly protects lives.
