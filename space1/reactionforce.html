The Strength You Give, Is What You Get In Return

June 11, 2025 Physics of Everyday Wonder

One of the best ways to understand physics is through real-time experiment. Not just big experiments, small mini-experiment could also offer us insights just as significant as the big ones. Evidently, one of my high school physics teachers agreed to that notion. There was one time—when I didn’t even see it coming—but he was showing us directly how physics works in our life. Even better, he did it in such a charming funny way. Well, at least to me, it was indeed charming and funny.

Here's the story. In the middle of a physics class, he invited one of my friends to stand up in the front of the class. That friend of mine was then directed to stand next to the classroom wall—as close as possible so that from one side of his body from shoulder to foot touched the wall. I don’t remember this detail, but he (my friend) probably tried to balance himself at first, like by rising his hand to certain heights, or, my teacher held him at first. What I remember vividly was the next seconds after that: neither my teacher held my friend nor my friend balanced himself. He was just standing exactly by the wall for a split second, then—he just fell down to his other side! Both us, the other students and my volunteer friend were shocked. For a moment there, I wondered why. So, now that I suppose I’ve understood the physics behind it—let’s break it down!

Just as my teacher back then explained--the moment of my friend falling with no one pushing him—was simply a proof of the Newton’s Third Law of Motion. This law is also commonly known as action-reaction law. It states that if a force acts upon an object, the object will react by exerting an equal force in opposite direction (to the force source). Looking back at that moment I witnessed my classmate fell, we didn’t see anything or anyone pushing my friend’s body, yet believe it or not, he was being pushed. When my friend’s body was pressing the wall, his body exerted a force acting on the wall. Then, as said by the Newton’s Third Law of Motion, the wall exerted a force of the equal strength in the opposite direction. In this case, the exerted force from the wall was directed to my friend’s body—opposing the force his body gave on the wall. Thus, along with the absence of balance and friction, my friend fell.

In a nutshell, my friend’s body pushed the wall (action), so the wall reacted by pushing back with an equal amount of force toward my friend (opposite direction). Meanwhile, nothing was supporting my friend from the other side to counter the reaction force, and his position couldn’t be balanced. As a result, he ended up falling. And yeah, you can’t simply destroy a wall, it’ll ‘destroy’ you instead, haha. I suppose everything about it is clear and understandable now. This case was indeed a simple physics law, but we can see that the effect could be massive. A closing reminder from me: don’t punch something firm when you’re furious, it’ll punch you back.
