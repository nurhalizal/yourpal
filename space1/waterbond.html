The (Nearly) Unbreakable Bond of Water Molecules

June 18, 2025 Physics of Everyday Wonder

I’m gonna start this piece by sharing a story of my first-time using genAI, specifically CGPT. During my first term as an undergraduate physics student, I got a task to write any question related to physics, or basically about anything and we need to write down the physics explanation of it. But there was a little twist: we were not supposed to generate our answers, we were told to ask ChatGPT for an answer. I faced a struggle of finding any question back then. Although, I do believe that we can ask the physics behind anything. Eventually, I came up with several questions, one of which is what I am going to write something related to.

My question sounded like this, “Why does waterflow tend to create a parabolic path/trajectory?” It made me curious since it’s a real thing we see daily, isn’t it? Furthermore, I guess we can expand the question by asking why water can create spherical droplets or why does a droplet of water cling to the tap before dripping. The answer to these questions is some physics principles; surface tension, cohesion, and even gravity and air resistance. Now, I invite you to walk down the physics explanation. By the way, this explanation based on my understanding of things I learn, the GPT answer I got back then, and some browsing.

First of all, for my initial question which I turned in as my assignment—both gravity, air resistance, surface tension and cohesion—are all at work! As we know, as water flows/pours, the gravity pulls it downward. Meanwhile, air resistance tries to constrain the flow, or in other words, we may say that the air resistance tries pushing the water upward against the gravity force. Besides, water is exceptionally cohesive—just as the internet says. In physics, cohesion is the tendency of the same molecules to stick to each other. The molecules we’re talking about are water molecules—which have strong hydrogen bonds between one molecule to another. The strong bonds give rise to high cohesion between water molecules, which later create surface tension of the water. As the water molecules firmly attract each other, the water liquid tends to minimize its surface area. As a result, waterflow creates such a well-structured parabolic path. This parabolic trajectory is a balanced form of the waterflow: the water still falls down due to gravity, but takes the farthest path possible with a ‘perfect’ shape due to air resistance and surface tension. As we can see, the parabolic water flow looks like the water is holding on to each other tightly.

Aside from the waterflow, the cohesion and surface tension also explain the droplet and tap dripping mysteries. The droplet is formed as a result of cohesive characteristic and surface tension of water. The spherical droplet shape allows the water molecules to hold on to each other tightly while also minimizing their surface area. The same cohesive effect applies to the dripping water. From my point of view, cohesion leads the droplet of water to also be highly attached to the water molecules that are still stuck on the tap until eventually gravity wins them over.

Wow, I don’t know about you, but writing and thinking through this fascinate me again and again. Come to think of it, even water molecules hold on to each other. Why can’t us, humans, do the same in terms of caring for each other?

PS: Without us realizing, the properties of water we just talked about benefit us in our everyday life. For a quick example: if we spill a glass of water, it would spread right away on the floor without the cohesion. And oh yeah, apparently, the Merlion statue in Singapore utilizes the properties above too.
