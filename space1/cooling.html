How Things Cool Down

June 25, 2026 Physics of Everyday Wonder

In our everyday lives, it’s absolutely common to come across temperature changing moments—either heating up or cooling down something whether we intend to or not. Changing temperature involves what we call as heat transfer. Heat is a form of energy which is naturally being transferred from higher temperature matter to lower temperature one. The reason behind this is that higher temperature corresponds to higher amount of heat within. Thus, to reach thermal equilibrium—as described by the Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics—heat is transferred from higher temperature to lower temperature. With this being said, it means that every open system with higher temperature that its surroundings will naturally cool down.

But, do you ever notice that, the way things cool down don’t always the same—typically the cooling time. Like how hot water takes longer time cool down to a certain temperature when you just put it on your desk compared to when you put it in the refrigerator. Of course, this sounds as normal as a common sense—because refrigerator is cold anything within will cool down quickly. But apart from that, did you know that in the same room temperature, hot water takes longer time to cool down from 50°C to 40°C compared to from 80°C to 70°C—it’s the same 10-degree range of temperature change! And it’s not even merely a ‘what if’, it’s a proven experiment that I myself have also tried.

In one of my basic physics’ experiments earlier, we boiled water and oil then let it cool down under room temperature. We took notes of how long it took for the water and oil to cool down every ten degrees Celsius. The result was as I mentioned above; they cooled down faster at higher temperature. If we hadn’t been given a module to learn beforehand, I would have been totally left in wonder, like, “How could it possibly cool down faster at high temperature even though it’s further from the room temperature?” Turned out, that high temperature difference was exactly the answer. In physics, this case is explained by the Newton’s Law of Cooling; the rate of temperature change of an object is proportional to the temperature difference between the object and its surrounding. Basically, when the temperature difference between system/object and its surrounding is higher, the quicker the temperature will change.

If we look back at our first example; the temperature difference between the hot water and the refrigerator is higher than the difference between the same water and the room temperature. As a result, it cools down quicker in the refrigerator. The same thing happened to the water and oil in my class experiment. At high temperature (around 80° C - 70°C), it’s obviously much further from the room temperature (around 27°C) compared to when it’s at around 50°C to 40°C. Therefore, we need a few extra seconds to cool the water down even when it’s already closer to the room temperature.

So, what do you think now? Everything makes sense now? Honestly, when I first learned about this, this rather simple thing amazed me to the core. Knowing this cooling law kinda gives me extra reason to put things on the freezer to cool them down quickly—despite already doing so before. But now, when I do that kind of thing, I can say to myself, “It’s gonna work, physics has proved it!”
