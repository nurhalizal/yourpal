Beyond The Delayed Thunder

July 23, 2025 Physics of Everyday Wonder

In a rainy season, we often catch sight of lightning—sometimes it even feels like we’re being photographed with an intense flashlight. But instead of posing as if a photo was being taken, people will cover their ears almost instantly as they notice a flash of lightning. Then seconds later—boom! The thunder arrives. Yes, over time, we’ve come to realize that lightning and thunder are related and likely to occur concurrently. It’s like they’re more or less the same thing that I even used to confuse the terms of these two in my own native language, Indonesian. I guess I started to be able to distinguish the terms in English than Indonesian, a language I was born to learn.

Well, enough about my personal fun fact—let’s get back to the lightning x thunder (almost sounds like an anime title, by the way). Just as I described earlier, despite the fact that thunder and lightning essentially occur at the same time, we always perceive the thunder a bit later after a lightning. Obviously, it’s another thing to wonder about, “Why does the thunder always come late?”

While the explanation of how they form seems quite complicated, the reason behind the thunder delay is pretty simple. But after a little browsing, let me try to explain the phenomena in a hopefully simple yet valid way. Thunder and lightning are parts of thunderstorm. Within the clouds, charge separation occurs followed by charge build-up (as in static electricity) which eventually cause a significant potential difference between parts of the cloud or between the cloud and the ground. This leads to rapid flows of electric current between the cloud and ground as the negative charge seeks an instant path to reach a positively charge spot (usually the ground). This occurrence is what creates the visible flash of light we know as lightning. Alongside the lightning formation, it heats the air intensely—rapid thermal expansion then creating a shock wave which we hear as the thunder. Now I suppose this elaboration just clarifies that thunder and lightning are formed simultaneously—which leads us back to our initial wonder, why is the thunder delayed?

So basically, lightning is the flash we see meanwhile thunder is the loud sound we hear. Both light and sound are waves—disturbances that propagate. The issue is, light and sound have different speed of propagation. Light travels so much faster than soundwave. In a vacuum, the speed of light is approximately 3 × 108 m/s (three hundred million meters per second), meanwhile soundwave only travels 340 m/s in the air. Such a distinct difference, right?

For that reason, we see the lightning instantly as it occurs regardless of the distance, while the sound of thunder needs some time to reach our ears depending on how far we are from the source. Based on that fact, using some calculations, the distance of the source/storm can be estimated. Somehow, I always find this kind of stuffs to be fascinating.

Oh, by the way, to end this piece, I want to share my recent thought on this that just came to my head as I’m writing but I’ll turn it into another kind of thing to wonder about. Don’t you think the thunder delay actually benefit us? It’s like the lightning was a signal sent for us to cover our ears or at least get prepared to be shocked. Perhaps it’s another piece of evidence of how precise this world was created.
