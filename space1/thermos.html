A Tale of A Thermos Bottle

May 21, 2025 Physics of Everyday Wonder

I love drinking iced drink, especially creamy coffee and Milo. For that reason, I bought a new tumbler earlier this year—a thermally insulated one. Yep, I bought a thermos bottle so that I can make my iced drink and bring it with me anywhere I go. Now I’ve been bringing the thermos bottle almost every time I go to campus for a while. But sometimes, I don’t even finish the drink before I got home. Like that one day (several days ago), I still had like a half bottle of Milo when I was already home. So, without thinking too much, I decided to place the thermos bottle in the refrigerator—hoping it will become colder. Hours after that, I took the bottle to drink up my Milo—just to realize that it actually made no sense to try cooling a drink that’s inside a thermos. But why do I say it’s nonsensical? Isn’t making things cold the function of a refrigerator?

Well, when I drank the Milo again after left it hours in the refrigerator, it didn’t get any colder. Meanwhile, I was sure that the refrigerator as well as the outside of the bottle were colder. But why couldn’t my Milo become any colder? So, what happened is—eh wait, I need to clarify this first; I haven’t gone through a further observation or browsing to make sure. This is kind of just what I think as the explanation behind this happening.

That time, I realized that it was just how a thermos works. A thermos is a thermally insulated container—which means it isolates the content within from external thermal influence. The thermos wall, which typically consists of three layers; inner wall-vacuum-outer wall acts as a barrier between the inside and its outside. The inside or content of the bottle is like the system, while the bottle’s surrounding is the environment. The layers of wall of the thermos keep the environment from affecting the content’s temperature, by preventing thermal transfer between them. That way, inside a thermos, hot water will stay hot meanwhile cold water will stay cold. But yes, if it’s there for a long time, the temperature will likely change eventually, possibly cause the insulator obviously isn’t perfect.

Anyway, that’s how it works. If the content inside a thermos is hot (has a big amount of heat), the heat will not be transferred into the environment. Thus, very little to no temperature change occur inside the thermos bottle. The same thing but the opposite happens with cold drink. The cold content stays cold because the thermos walls block heat from the environment to enter the bottle. So, for my case I mentioned, my milo inside has a higher temperature compared to the refrigerator—which means more heat is preserved within the thermos. The only way to make the drink cooler was by letting it transfer heat to its surrounding—the refrigerator. However, as my milo was inside a thermally insulated bottle, the heat transfer couldn’t occur. As a result, the temperature of my milo remained the same. I’m not 100$\%$ sure if this explanation is valid, but hopefully it is. I’ll try look it up next time!

By the way, do you also ever wonder why the ice of cold drink still melts even inside a thermos. Well, the thermos prevents heat transfer between the outside and the content within, and doesn’t do anything with heat transfer inside. If there are ice and liquid inside a thermos, it’s obvious that the liquid has higher temperature—more heat. So, they ‘blend’ to reach thermal equilibrium anyway, even inside a thermos. This will result in the melting of the ice as it gets heat from the liquid.

Sometimes, things seem weirdly not going as we expected, but it’s actually just physics at play.
