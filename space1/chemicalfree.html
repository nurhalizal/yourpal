What's 'Chemical-Free'?

August 10, 2025 Science of Everyday Awe

‘Chemical-free’ that’s what they said in certain products’ labels and advertisements. But how is that even possible? Water is chemical; so are caffeine and vitamins—and even the oxygen we breathe in is chemical. So, what exactly do they mean when a label says, ‘chemical-free’? Usually, certain products are considered chemical-free if they’re organic or natural based, hence they’re assumed to be safe. But apparently, there’s a layer of misconception about this. To try and solve this common misunderstanding, let’s break down what chemical, organic, and nature actually mean.

In science, ‘chemical’ is defined as any substance with particular definite composition—either they’re made of a single type of element, or a compound of some. Oxygen, gold, silver are some examples of single element chemicals, whilst water, sugar, and caffeine that I’ve mentioned are compounds. So basically, just as we can see from the examples—everything that has matter is made of chemicals.

Moving on, we’re also better off knowing what organic and natural are. Organic itself can have different meanings based on the contexts. For so long, I’ve always thought of organic substance as anything that’s found in or extracted from living things. Until today, I still think that’s not totally wrong, yet there are more proper contextual definitions of it. In science, there’s a branch of chemistry called organic chemistry. This particular study deals with chemical compounds that primarily consists of carbon, which often bond with hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen.

To be fair, organic compounds are indeed fundamental to life and living organisms—yet they can also be synthetic, take plastic as an example. Then aside from that, in food or agriculture, organic also has another meaning; it refers to farming processes that prioritize ecological balance, by not using synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, and so on. So, when it comes to food/agriculture, organic is related to the practices/processes, not the composition of the products.

And then, how about natural? As the term suggests, it is defined as a characteristic of things occurring in nature without human interference. I suppose it’s safe to say that natural refers to something non-synthetic or non-artificial, but I highly doubt that it can be assumed as safe—let alone chemical free. Natural substance does contain chemicals, and can even be toxic.

Evidently, we may now see the misconception here. Both organic and natural materials are made of chemical compounds, hence nothing is chemical-free as long as it has matter. And even though organic and natural sound ‘non-artificial’, it doesn’t imply their safety—many can still be toxic. However, people colloquially define chemical as synthetic or artificial and potentially toxic.

Therefore, when a product’s label says ‘chemical-free’, they simply mean the product doesn’t contain artificial substances. But even so, it doesn’t guarantee its safety. Some ‘safe’ natural/organic substances can still be harmful for some. So from now on, don’t get these definitions mixed up.
