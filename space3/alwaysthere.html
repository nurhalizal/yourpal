When It Isn't Always There

July 3, 2025 Random

There was a period of time—like for several years—where I used to have a little ‘practice’/habit every Raya day. As we came back from the mosque, I would ask my parents to buy me some ice cream, specifically ice cream cone. I was then taken to a minimarket to buy at least an ice cream cone. An ice cream cone seemed like a luxurious desert to me. It was something I didn’t want to ask from my parents a lot and even if I had some pocket money to buy one, I would likely re-think it lots of times just to end up not buying it. Hence, the Raya habit became a thing for me. I felt like the Eid day is one of the only few days I would have a chance to buy an ice cream cone. Basically, I limited myself from buying ice cream cones too much for reasons I can’t even fully explain now.

What’s interesting about it is the fact that the ice cream cone I had every Raya day was so special. I mean, it’s just a piece of ice cream we could find anywhere, so why did it felt so special to have one? Later on, I realized something; I valued (and still do, actually) the ice cream a lot because I like it yet I told myself I can’t always have it.

Apparently, I relate to the notion that something will become more precious if its presence is limited. I suppose the same thing goes for several other things. For instance, people tend to be excited to be in a holiday because we’re aware that our time for holiday is limited and we know that it is indeed enjoyable. Or, imagine unable to consume sugar for a certain period of time—the first bite of cake you eat afterwards would taste more than delicious even if it’s just the same cake you normally had before. I guess these examples are quite enough to support my opinion. Besides, I’ve even got a number of other instances in my thought.

I know that not everyone will agree on this, yet I find it highly relatable. Honestly, I am also curious of how or why do we feel that way. Thinking of this, I suppose it has something to do with a bit of our nature as human. Generally, everyone wants positive things that we’re fond of last long—or in terms of presence, we want those good stuffs to always be around. Consequently, when we know that a particular good thing has a very limited time with us, or we’re limited to have it around—we’ll instinctively value it even more. I bet we’ll try making the best out of it, enjoy it as much as we can, and be highly appreciative of it.

Even so, this doesn’t mean that the presence of something is the mere indicator to determine how precious the thing is. Other aspects obviously play their roles about the value of certain things. However, I believe that the presence does matter. The presence I mean here is either its presence in the whole world or its presence for us. Well, limited edition stuffs are usually more valued, right? Besides, even an ordinary thing can become very special simply because it’s not always there for us.
