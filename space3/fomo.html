Is FOMO positive?

July 12, 2025 Random

Seemingly, the significant growth and popularity of the internet and social also influence our mentality universally. One well-known form of this impact is the fear of missing out—FOMO. Based on its general definition, FOMO is the anxiety of being left out, typically in terms of social aspect. People who experience this anxiety tend to be highly worried that they’ve been missing lots of things—important events, updates, and opportunities.

Having this feeling leads people to think that they’re far behind of everyone else in life. To people with FOMO, everyone else’s life seems to be far more exciting and fuller of joy compared to their own which also makes them feel like being excluded socially. The reason beyond this social phenomenon is basically related to our nature to seek for social connection, hence we feel the need to be ‘equal’ with others to build connection.

Then, in the middle of this need, social media adds fuel to the burning of FOMO. Social media allows us to ‘see’ shared experience of others limitlessly. But instead of showing us the reality, social media is subtly becoming a platform to create the illusion of the perfect life, as well as to share any ‘interesting’ updates—which at times unnecessary. Consequently, people constantly compare their lives with others, especially to those who they find to have joyous life. With all these, the fear of missing out have begun to arise among a bunch of people.

From this point of view, we can see that FOMO is certainly a negative issue we’re facing in the meantime. Experiencing FOMO will lead to the decrease of life gratitude and satisfaction, while increasing stress and even depression. Besides, without us realizing, it could also limit us from our own freedom of life, and the worst part of it in my thoughts: sooner or later it will affect our physical conditions.

For all these reasons, we are sure in need to cope this social issue. But, how? First of all, I believe that reducing our social media screen time will help as it’s actually a source of this anxiety itself. If you feel like social media has started overwhelming you with anxiety, leaving it at all might be a big help. Besides, it’s apparently important to realize that every one doesn’t have to be at the same level—it’s normal not to know certain things, not to follow some particular things most people are into, and it’s absolutely normal to have our own path and pace in life. Having different source of happiness doesn’t mean we do not own it. Then, we also need to acknowledge the FOMO as a kind of temporary reaction or feeling which will eventually fade away if we don’t get too meddled with it.

But on top of that, I somehow have a thought that FOMO—or at least the words shouldn’t always indicate negativity. What if we turn this fear-of-missing-out into a good matter. I’m convinced that there are cases where FOMO could be a positive thing to have. Rather than fearing of being left out in terms of trends or fun, we better shift our focus inward: what parts of us we’ve been leaving behind? While some are too focused trying to catch up with people they barely know on social media, let us start paying more attention to real things around us, like making sure we’re not leaving ourselves unhealed inside. There may be parts of ourselves we’ve been neglecting, things within that we should improve, relatives around we should’ve checked on more, and even people around us that we should’ve looked up to instead. If we turn typical FOMO into the fear-of-missing-out of our goodness, I’m optimistic that we’ll have a better chance of living our life to the fullest without worrying of things we didn’t even need to think of in the first place.

Ps: I’m not sure if I’ve arranged my words and sentences in the best way possible, but hopefully my point is understandable and actually makes sense.
